# When Multi-Agent Fails

Research reference for *Agentic Loops*, Chapter 14 (third and final chapter of Part III; the reality-check capstone of the multi-agent arc). Current as of 2026. The reader already knows the delegation mechanic and the coordination topologies; this chapter is the critical, empirically-grounded failure analysis and the honest case for when NOT to build multi-agent. Do not cheerlead. Benchmark figures are often vendor-reported or benchmark-specific; version-pin and re-verify.

## TL;DR
- Multi-agent LLM systems fail at strikingly high rates — 41% to 86.7% across seven popular open-source frameworks in the MAST study — and the largest single share of those failures (44.2%) is bad system design, not weak models. The empirical record says: do not adopt multi-agent by default; earn it.
- The failure is architectural, not just model-limited. Failures persist across GPT-4, Claude 3, Qwen2.5, and CodeLlama, and MAST's authors explicitly conjecture that better base models alone will be insufficient — the problem is organizational (poor roles, lost context, weak verification, error propagation), and multi-agent systems burn ~15× the tokens of a single chat while being far harder to debug.
- A narrow class genuinely works: read-heavy, breadth-first parallelizable tasks (Anthropic's research system beat single-agent Opus 4 by 90.2%) and setups where writes stay single-threaded and extra agents contribute read-only intelligence (Cognition's revised position). Tightly-coupled, shared-context, write-heavy tasks — most coding — remain a poor fit.

## The empirical failure picture
The best empirical answer comes from **MAST — the Multi-Agent System Failure Taxonomy** ("Why Do Multi-Agent LLM Systems Fail?", Cemri et al., UC Berkeley, arXiv:2503.13657; v1 March 2025, v3 October 2025; accepted NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks). The field's first rigorous bottom-up study of *why* these systems break rather than *whether* they score well.

**Methodology.** MAST-Data: 1,642 annotated execution traces (each averaging 15,000+ lines) from 7 frameworks — ChatDev, MetaGPT, HyperAgent, AppWorld, AG2 (MathChat), Magentic-One, OpenManus — run with GPT-4o, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Qwen2.5-Coder, CodeLlama, across coding, math, and general-agent tasks. The taxonomy was built via Grounded Theory from 150 traces by six expert annotators (20+ hours each), validated to **Cohen's κ = 0.88**. To scale, an LLM-as-a-Judge on o1 was calibrated to **94% accuracy and κ = 0.77** against humans.

**Headline finding.** Failure rates ran **41% to 86.7%**. Because each framework is measured on its own benchmark, the numbers are not directly comparable, but uniformly sobering: AppWorld (Test-C) 86.7% failure; HyperAgent (SWE-Bench Lite) 74.7%; ChatDev (ProgramDev) 66.7% (only 33.3% correctness); Magentic-One (GAIA) ~62%; MetaGPT (ProgramDev) ~60%; AG2/MathChat (OlympiadBench) 41% (the best). The paper's epigraph adapts Tolstoy: "Successful systems all work alike; each failing system has its own problems."

**The 14 failure modes in 3 categories** (prevalence across the full 1,642-trace dataset):
- **FC1 — Specification and System Design Issues (44.2%):** disobey task specification (11.8%), disobey role specification (1.5%), step repetition (15.7%), loss of conversation history (2.8%), unaware of termination conditions (12.4%).
- **FC2 — Inter-Agent Misalignment (32.3%):** conversation reset (2.2%), fail to ask for clarification (6.8%), task derailment (7.4%), information withholding (0.85%), ignored other agent's input (1.9%), reasoning-action mismatch (13.2%).
- **FC3 — Task Verification and Termination (23.5%):** premature termination (6.2%), no or incomplete verification (8.2%), incorrect verification (9.1%).

The distribution is deliberately balanced — no single category dominates. (On the 210-trace balanced subset in Figure 4 the split is 41.8% / 36.9% / 21.3%; the ordering FC1 > FC2 > FC3 holds in both.) The three most-common individual modes — step repetition, reasoning-action mismatch, unaware of termination — are none of them "the model wasn't smart enough" failures.

## Context and communication failures
FC2 is where the coordination mechanics go wrong. MAST's canonical example (Figure 3) is **information withholding**: in AppWorld, a Phone Agent knows the login "username" field actually requires the account phone number but never tells the Supervisor, who fails to ask for clarification — producing repeated failed logins and task failure. The information existed; it never reached the agent that needed it.

This is exactly the problem Cognition's Walden Yan formalized in **"Don't Build Multi-Agents"** (June 2025). Two principles: **(1) Share context, and share full agent traces, not just individual messages. (2) Actions carry implicit decisions, and conflicting decisions carry bad results.**

The **Flappy Bird example** operationalizes both. Decompose "build a Flappy Bird clone" into Subtask 1 ("moving game background with green pipes and hit boxes") and Subtask 2 ("a bird you can move up and down"), farm to two subagents, combine. Naive version: subagent 1 builds a Super Mario Bros-style background while subagent 2 builds a bird that neither looks like a game asset nor moves like Flappy Bird. Even after copying full task context to both, you get a bird and background in **completely different visual styles** — because each made unstated implicit decisions (art style, physics) that silently conflicted. Yan's conclusion: rule out by default any architecture violating Principles 1 & 2, because "the decision-making ends up being too dispersed and context isn't able to be shared thoroughly enough between the agents."

MAST's Insight 2 sharpens the pessimism: solutions focused merely on context or communication protocols are "often insufficient" for FC2 failures, which demand deeper "theory of mind" — one agent modeling another's informational needs. Base LLMs are not pre-trained for that.

## Coordination and error propagation
The deeper structural problem: **errors compound rather than cancel.** When agents consume each other's outputs, a single hallucination becomes an accepted premise downstream — a "soft failure" with no stack trace, propagating silently as reasoning drift. Agent B trusts A; C builds on B's work embedding A's error; by the time it's visible, the root cause is buried in confident, well-formatted prose.

**The reliability math is unforgiving.** Reliability compounds multiplicatively: five 99%-reliable components → 95% system reliability; ten → 90%. A five-agent pipeline where each passes data correctly 90% of the time yields 0.9^5 ≈ 59% end-to-end; two agents at the same rate give 81%. Every handoff multiplies error probability, so the single most effective reliability intervention is often shortening the chain. Kim et al. ("Towards a Science of Scaling Agent Systems," Google Research/DeepMind & MIT, arXiv:2512.08296): "independent agents amplify errors 17.2× through unchecked propagation, while centralized coordination contains this to 4.4×."

**More agents can mean less reliability.** Chen, Davis, Hanin, Bailis, Stoica, Zaharia, Zou (NeurIPS 2024, "Are More LLM Calls All You Need?", arXiv:2403.02419) showed even simple Vote/Filter-Vote systems have *non-monotonic* scaling: performance "can first increase but then decrease as a function of the number of LM calls," because "more LM calls lead to higher performance on 'easy' queries, but lower performance on 'hard' queries." Kim et al.'s 180 configurations found the same asymmetry: "Centralized coordination improves performance by 80.9% on parallelizable tasks like financial reasoning… Yet for sequential reasoning tasks, every multi-agent variant we tested degraded performance by 39–70%" (PlanCraft up to −50.4% centralized, −41.4% decentralized, −39.0% hybrid). They quantify a saturation threshold: coordination "yields diminishing or negative returns (β=−0.408, p<0.001) once single-agent baselines exceed an empirical threshold of ∼45%" — the smarter your base agent already is, the less coordination helps and the more it can hurt. Silo-Bench (2026): single-agent performance loss of 15–49% even at team size k=2, compounding to 80–100% at k=50 on harder tasks.

**Other pathologies** (MAST + literature): task derailment (7.4%); duplicated work (Anthropic's early agents spawned 50 subagents for simple queries; three redundantly investigated the same chip-shortage sub-question); infinite retry loops and deadlocks (A fails → triggers B's error handler → which recalls A); retry storms / thundering herd; conformity/echo-chamber effects in debate (a confident-but-wrong agent sways the group). Debate research is itself equivocal: Zhang et al. (ICLR 2025 blogpost, "Stop Overvaluing Multi-Agent Debate," arXiv:2502.08788) found across five frameworks and nine benchmarks that "MAD methods fail to reliably outperform simple single-agent baselines such as Chain-of-Thought and Self-Consistency, even when consuming additional inference-time computation" (Society-of-Minds underperformed CoT on all nine with GPT-4o-mini).

## The reliability and cost reality
Even where multi-agent *works*, it's expensive and brittle. Anthropic: **agents use ~4× the tokens of a chat; multi-agent systems ~15×.** On BrowseComp, "token usage by itself explains 80% of the variance, with the number of tool calls and the model choice as the two other explanatory factors" (95% combined) — much of multi-agent's benefit is simply "spending enough tokens," which you pay for whether or not the task decomposes. Anthropic is explicit: "for economic viability, multi-agent systems require tasks where the value of the task is high enough to pay for the increased performance." A single deep-research session can run to millions of tokens; the economics only close for high-value work (legal due diligence, competitive intelligence, biomedical literature review).

**Debugging is qualitatively harder.** Agents are non-deterministic between runs even at temperature 0; replaying a request often fails to reproduce the failure. Anthropic reports users complaining agents were "not finding obvious information" with no visible cause — resolved only by full production tracing. Their blunt summary: "minor changes cascade into large behavioral changes," "the last mile often becomes most of the journey," and "one step failing can cause agents to explore entirely different trajectories." Debugging causality "spreads across multiple agents, so stack traces point everywhere and nowhere." This is the demo-to-production gap — which MAST attributes largely to design and Anthropic to the compounding, stateful nature of long-running agents.

## The single-agent counterargument in full
Cognition's case: in 2025 the models are extremely intelligent, so the binding constraint is **context engineering** — "effectively the #1 job of engineers building AI agents" — not agent count. A single-threaded linear agent keeps context continuous and never makes conflicting implicit decisions, satisfying both principles for free, and "will get you very far." When context overflows, the recommended escalation is not parallel agents but a dedicated model that compresses history into key decisions/events (Cognition fine-tuned a smaller model for this) — keeping one coherent thread.

Yan's read on frameworks is pointed: OpenAI's Swarm and Microsoft's AutoGen "actively push concepts which I believe to be the wrong way of building agents," and multi-agent collaboration in 2025 "only results in fragile systems." Tellingly, as of June 2025 **Claude Code's subagents never write code in parallel** — they answer well-defined questions, precisely to avoid conflicting decisions; the benefit is context isolation, not parallel authorship. Recent academic work echoes this: a 2026 study found single-agent LLMs *outperform* multi-agent systems on multi-hop reasoning under equal thinking-token budgets, and industry analyses report single-agent setups beating multi-agent on sequential reasoning where coordination overhead outweighs parallelization benefit.

Synthesis point: **many multi-agent architectures are solving self-inflicted problems.** Fragmentation, misalignment, and conflicting decisions are *created* by the decomposition; a single coherent context never has them. Even Anthropic — multi-agent's most credible champion — draws the same boundary: "domains that require all agents to share the same context or involve many dependencies between agents are not a good fit for multi-agent systems today. For instance, most coding tasks involve fewer truly parallelizable tasks than research."

## When multi-agent helps vs doesn't — balanced synthesis
The tension resolves cleanly once you separate *reads* from *writes*.

**Right choice when** (per Anthropic): breadth-first and parallelizable (many independent directions at once); information exceeds a single window; sub-tasks cleanly, independently decomposable; high-value enough to absorb ~15× token cost; read-heavy (search, research, compression). Anthropic's orchestrator-worker research system (Opus 4 lead planning + 3–5 Sonnet 4 subagents with isolated windows + a separate CitationAgent) "outperformed single-agent Claude Opus 4 by 90.2% on our internal research eval" and cut research time up to 90%. Canonical win: "identify all the board members of the companies in the Information Technology S&P 500" — decomposes trivially while a single agent grinds through sequential searches.

**Wrong choice when**: tightly coupled, needing shared context and coherent decisions; reliability paramount; a single agent suffices; real-time collaborative writing (multiple agents mutating one artifact). This is most coding — why both Cognition and MAST converge on caution.

**Emerging nuanced consensus** — Cognition's April 2026 follow-up "Multi-Agents: What's Actually Working," important precisely as a *reversal-with-conditions* from the same author. Through-line: **"multi-agent systems work best today when writes stay single-threaded and the additional agents contribute intelligence rather than actions."** Working patterns: **clean-context generator-verifier loops** (even on Devin-written PRs, "Devin Review catches an average of 2 bugs per PR, of which roughly 58% are severe" — and works *best when coder and reviewer share no context beforehand*, the reviewer's clean context sidestepping Context Rot); **"Smart Friend" escalation** (a weaker primary calling a frontier model, or cross-frontier routing — worked across strong frontier models, *failed* with an asymmetrically weaker primary, an open training problem); **manager-worker "map-reduce-and-manage"** (a manager Devin shards scoped work to child Devins over internal MCP, then synthesizes — the Devin Security Swarm's "Agentic MapReduce" hit 72% recall on real CVEs). Cognition is blunt that unstructured swarms — "arbitrary networks of agents negotiating with each other" — are "mostly a distraction." Principle: don't reach for multi-agent by default; earn it, and keep the writer single-threaded.

## Mitigations and best practices
For teams that have earned it, the research points to concrete, non-optional practices:
- **Share full context and full traces, not just messages** (Cognition Principle 1). If an action can't see all relevant prior decisions, expect conflicting implicit decisions.
- **Keep writes single-threaded.** Funnel state mutations through one point; additional agents contribute read-only intelligence. The single highest-leverage architectural rule.
- **Specify roles and tasks precisely.** MAST's ChatDev case study: improving agent role specification alone (ensuring the CEO agent had final say) yielded **+9.4%** task success on the same model and prompt. Anthropic: without detailed task descriptions, "agents duplicate work, leave gaps, or fail to find necessary information."
- **Build multi-level verification.** MAST's Insight 3: sole reliance on final-stage, low-level checks is inadequate — a ChatDev chess program passed compilation but failed at runtime because nothing validated game rules. Adding a high-level task-objective verification step (with a DAG→cyclic topology change enabling iterative refinement) produced the study's largest single gain, **+15.6%**. But verifiers are "not a silver bullet" — presence doesn't guarantee reliability if checks are superficial.
- **Robust termination and error recovery.** "Unaware of termination conditions" is 12.4%; premature termination another 6.2%. Add explicit termination criteria, max-iteration cutoffs, retry caps with exponential backoff, dead-letter queues, checkpointing, resume-not-restart (Anthropic combines model adaptability with "deterministic safeguards like retry logic and regular checkpoints").
- **Circuit breakers and cost caps.** Published architectures often lack per-run caps; a recursively-spawning subagent or oversized tool result can multiply cost 10×+.
- **Full-trace observability.** Non-determinism makes failures hard to reproduce; you need distributed tracing across agents (runs, traces, threads), decision-pattern monitoring, replay. A team that "shrug[s] and say[s] the model is non-deterministic" is "operating a production system blind."
- **Evaluation designed for non-determinism.** No single "correct" path — evaluate outcomes and reasonableness of process, not prescribed steps. Anthropic's playbook: start with ~20 realistic queries; LLM-as-judge with an explicit rubric (factual accuracy, citation accuracy, completeness, source quality, tool efficiency); humans in the loop for edge cases. Beware pass@1 overstating reliability.
- **Structured orchestration over emergent, where reliability matters.** MAST's deepest reframing (Insight 1): "MAS failure is not merely a function of challenges in the underlying model; a well-designed MAS can result in performance gain when using the same underlying model." Building a good multi-agent system is like designing a good organization — even organizations of brilliant individuals fail catastrophically if the structure is flawed.

## The forward-looking view
**Will better base models solve this?** MAST's authors think not entirely — their most consequential claim: "we conjecture that improvements in the base model capabilities will be insufficient to address the full MAST. Instead, we argue that good MAS design requires organizational understanding — even organizations of sophisticated individuals can fail catastrophically if the organization structure is flawed." Failure patterns persisted across GPT-4, Claude 3, Qwen2.5, CodeLlama — architectural, not purely capability-bound. Some modes *are* model-limited (theory-of-mind, communicative intelligence may improve with targeted training), but role clarity, verification, and termination are design problems scaling won't fix.

**What is improving:** models becoming more natively agentic (Cognition observed ~8× Devin usage growth in cautious enterprise over six months); interoperability protocols maturing (Google's **A2A**, launched April 9 2025, now under the Linux Foundation with 150+ supporters, HTTP/SSE/JSON-RPC 2.0 with Agent Cards, complementing MCP); better evaluation/observability tooling, verification methods, and structured patterns (map-reduce-and-manage, generator-verifier loops). MAST cautions that even MCP and A2A — standardizing *how* messages pass — don't fix the *content* problem of agents failing to model each other's informational needs.

**Honest uncertainty.** The counter-argument that today's high failure rates reflect immature tooling rather than a fundamental limit has genuine merit — Cognition's own reversal (from "don't build multi-agents" to "here's the narrow class that works") is evidence the frontier is moving. But the direction of travel is telling: the patterns that work are the *disciplined, structured, single-writer* ones, and unstructured swarms remain "a distraction." Measured conclusion: multi-agent is a powerful tool for a genuinely narrow set of read-heavy, parallelizable, high-value problems, and a reliability liability everywhere else. Treat it as an engineering problem with known failure modes and known mitigations — not a vibe, and not a default.

## Recommendations (staged)
1. **Default to a single, well-contexted agent.** Before decomposing, ask whether one agent with strong context engineering, good tools, and a long/compacted window suffices. If so, stop — you avoid the entire FC1/FC2 failure surface. Reconsider only when required information reliably exceeds a single effective window, or sequential latency is unacceptable.
2. **Only go multi-agent when the task is read-heavy, breadth-first parallelizable, cleanly decomposable, and high-value.** Use the Anthropic profile as the positive template and the ~15× multiplier as the economic hurdle. If the task has heavy inter-agent dependencies or shared write state, stay single-agent.
3. **If you must coordinate agents, keep writes single-threaded.** Additional agents contribute read-only intelligence (search, review, escalation). Adopt map-reduce-and-manage over unstructured swarms. The highest-leverage rule in the literature.
4. **Engineer the "organization," not just the prompts.** Specify roles precisely (~+9.4% in MAST's ChatDev study), add multi-level verification including high-level objective checks (~+15.6%), set explicit termination conditions and iteration caps.
5. **Instrument before you scale.** Full-trace observability, checkpoint/resume, retry caps, and circuit breakers are prerequisites, not afterthoughts. Evaluate with outcome-based rubrics and LLM-as-judge plus human spot-checks; don't trust pass@1.
6. **Measure end-to-end task success, not component uptime, and shorten the chain.** Every agent removed improves multiplicative reliability more than optimizing any single agent. If adding an agent doesn't raise end-to-end success on a held-out eval, remove it — and recall Kim et al.'s finding that coordination turns negative once single-agent baselines exceed ~45%.

## Caveats
- Per-framework failure rates are not directly comparable — each MAS was measured on a different benchmark, so "41–86.7%" describes a set of systems, not a controlled ranking.
- Some MAST figures shifted across versions (AG2's rate and exact category percentages differ between the ICLR workshop version, v1, and v3). This doc uses v3 / full-dataset figures (44.2/32.3/23.5) and flags the 210-trace subset (41.8/36.9/21.3) separately.
- Vendor sources have incentives: Cognition sells Devin, Anthropic sells Claude; both posts are simultaneously credible field reports and marketing. Their agreement on the *boundaries* (read-only/parallelizable good, shared-write/coupled bad) is more trustworthy than either's product claims. Anthropic's 90.2% and Cognition's "2 bugs per PR / 58% severe" are internal, not independently audited.
- The field is moving fast: Cognition's own position reversed within ~10 months; A2A and verification research are early. "Better models won't fix this" is a reasoned MAST conjecture, not settled; the immature-tooling counter-hypothesis remains live.
- Multi-agent debate is genuinely contested: some studies show gains on specific reasoning/judging tasks, others (Zhang et al., ICLR 2025) show it fails to beat single-agent baselines; results depend heavily on task, aggregation method, and agent diversity.
