# Retrieval as Memory

Research reference for *Agentic Loops*, Chapter 17 (third chapter of Part IV, Memory; follows "The Memory Taxonomy" and "Prompt Caching and the Economics of Remembering," precedes "Self-Managed Memory"). Current as of 2026. The reader already knows the memory taxonomy (working/episodic/semantic/procedural, vector stores and knowledge graphs, the lifecycle) and prompt caching (KV-cache prefix caching, the rule that dynamic/retrieved content goes at the END of the prompt to preserve the cached prefix). This chapter is the deep treatment of retrieval as the mechanism that moves information from long-term stores into the context window on demand — going deeper than an intro-to-RAG treatment. Fast-moving area; version-pin and re-verify. Note that several vendor benchmark numbers are contested.

## TL;DR
- Treat retrieval as the *read side* of an agent's long-term memory: persistent knowledge lives in external stores (vector DBs, knowledge graphs, document stores) and retrieval pulls the relevant fragments into the finite context window on demand. The engineering discipline is getting the *right* information into context at the *right* time — not maximizing what you stuff in.
- The 2026 production consensus is a layered pipeline: intelligent chunking (with contextual retrieval or late chunking), a strong embedding model, **hybrid dense+sparse retrieval fused with Reciprocal Rank Fusion**, and a **cross-encoder reranker** as a cheap precision-correction layer — wrapped in **agentic control** where the model decides when, what, and how to retrieve. Anthropic reported this stack cuts the top-20-chunk retrieval failure rate by 67% (5.7%→1.9%).
- Long context did not kill RAG; the two are complementary (retrieve *into* long context). RAG still wins on large/dynamic corpora for cost, latency, and precision (context rot / lost-in-the-middle), while long context wins on small, self-contained bodies of text. The live frontier is agentic/iterative retrieval, late-interaction and multimodal models (ColBERT/ColPali), and temporal memory graphs.

## Retrieval as the bridge from long-term to working memory
The prior chapters established the taxonomy and the KV-cache economics; this chapter is the mechanism connecting them. Retrieval moves information from external long-term stores into the context window (working memory) on demand. An agent's persistent knowledge — semantic (facts, documentation, KBs) and episodic (past conversations, tool outputs, prior trajectories) — is far larger than any window and lives in vector databases, knowledge graphs, and document stores. Retrieval is the *read* operation of the memory read–write cycle; the next chapter covers the write/consolidation side.

The core problem is a hard capacity-and-quality constraint. Even with million-token windows you cannot fit an enterprise corpus, and — critically — you *should not* try: "context rot" and "lost-in-the-middle" mean adding marginally-relevant text actively degrades answer quality. Retrieval's job is to select the minimal high-relevance subset per query. This reframes RAG from a document-QA trick into the central problem of agent memory: **precision of recall, not volume of recall.**

## The retrieval pipeline in depth
**Chunking / segmentation.**
- *Fixed-size* (token/character windows, 10–20% overlap): simple, cheap, surprisingly strong. Qu, Tu & Bao, "Is Semantic Chunking Worth the Computational Cost?" (Findings of ACL: NAACL 2025, pp. 2155–2177): fixed ~200-word chunks matched or beat semantic chunking on retrieval and answer generation; "the computational costs associated with semantic chunking are not justified by consistent performance gains."
- *Recursive* (split on structural separators — paragraphs → sentences): the LangChain default and best general-purpose balance; a Feb 2026 Vecta benchmark placed recursive 512-token splitting first at 69% accuracy vs 54% for semantic chunking (which produced ~43-token fragments).
- *Semantic* (embed sentences, cut at similarity troughs): groups coherent topics but is compute-heavy and can produce tiny fragments; inconsistent gains.
- *Sentence-window / parent-document (hierarchical)*: retrieve on a small precise unit, return a larger surrounding window — decouples retrieval precision from generation context.
- *Late chunking*: embed the entire document (or long span) first with a long-context model (e.g., jina-embeddings-v2, 8,192-token window), then split token embeddings and mean-pool per chunk. Each chunk embedding carries document-level context, fixing dangling pronouns ("Its more than 3.85 million inhabitants…" stays bound to "Berlin"). Higher efficiency than contextual retrieval but tends to sacrifice some relevance/completeness (Merola & Singh, arXiv:2504.19754).

Central tradeoff: smaller chunks → higher retrieval precision but risk splitting relevant info across boundaries (the chunk-boundary problem); larger chunks → preserve context but dilute the embedding and pollute the window. 2026 best practice: start with recursive ~512-token chunks + overlap, layer contextual retrieval on high-value documents, evaluate on your own corpus.

*Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL)* trains embeddings so any prefix of the vector is independently useful — important info packed into the earliest dimensions. Truncate (e.g., 3072→768) to cut storage and ANN cost with modest quality loss; OpenAI 3-large and Gemini ship with MRL. Pattern: search at low dimensions to shortlist, rescore top-K at full dimension. Recent work (arXiv:2605.16608): truncation robustness is partly inherent to learned representations; MRL's clear advantage only shows under heavy (~80%+) truncation.

**Vector search / ANN.** Exact NN is infeasible at scale, so ANN indexes trade controllable recall for speed:
- **HNSW** (graph-based, logarithmic scaling): the default; best speed/recall but memory-hungry.
- **IVF** (partitions the space): faster to build, lower memory, slightly lower recall.
- **DiskANN / StreamingDiskANN** (disk-resident graphs): larger-than-memory (Milvus, pgvectorscale).
- **ScaNN** (Google, anisotropic quantization): high throughput.

Metrics: cosine (default for normalized text embeddings), dot product, Euclidean. Landscape: dedicated engines (Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, Milvus, Turbopuffer, LanceDB), Postgres extensions (pgvector, pgvectorscale), embedded stores (Chroma, FAISS). Under ~10M vectors a standard HNSW setup suffices; beyond ~50–100M, purpose-built systems outperform extensions. One 2026 benchmark: Qdrant ~4ms p50 at 1M vectors; Turbopuffer's object-storage economics popular for large cost-sensitive deployments.

**Retrieval strategies.** Top-k baseline; similarity thresholds filter low-confidence hits; **MMR (Maximal Marginal Relevance)** trades relevance against diversity, reducing near-duplicate chunks that waste context budget. Metadata filtering (date, source, permissions) is essential and often decisive for agent memory ("facts about *this* user updated in *this* quarter").

## Beyond naive vector search — hybrid and advanced retrieval
**Hybrid search.** Dense captures paraphrase/concept; sparse (BM25, or learned sparse SPLADE) captures exact terms — product codes, error strings, names, rare jargon embeddings collapse together. Because score scales are incompatible, the standard fusion is **Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF)**: RRF(d) = Σ 1/(k + rank_r(d)), k=60 conventional. RRF operates on ranks, sidestepping calibration, and beats either retriever alone (WANDS e-commerce benchmark: basic RRF NDCG 0.7068 vs BM25 0.6983 and pure vector 0.7... ). Practical notes: on very small corpora lower k (a practitioner reported k=60 flattening an 80-doc rank list, fixed by k=10); precompute SPLADE document vectors at index time since query-time SPLADE adds 100–300ms.

**Reranking (two-stage retrieval).** First-stage bi-encoders embed query and document independently → measure topical similarity, not answerability. A cross-encoder reranker jointly attends over (query, document) pairs → far more accurate relevance scores, at O(candidates) model passes, so applied only to a shortlist. Pattern: retrieve top 50–200 → rerank → keep top 3–10. Leading rerankers: Cohere Rerank 3.5, Voyage rerank-2, open BAAI bge-reranker-v2-m3 (568M params, 100+ languages), Jina reranker v2. Latency ~20–100ms for tens of candidates on GPU (seconds on CPU). Rerankers "rescue" deeply-relevant documents first-stage retrieval buried (a truly relevant doc stranded at rank #45) and are the single highest-precision-per-dollar upgrade. Caveat: cross-encoders can underperform on identifier-heavy (code) queries where late-interaction wins, and reranking occasionally hurts on specific tasks — measure per task.

**Query transformation.** The query is often a poor probe:
- *Query rewriting / expansion*: reformulate into retrieval-friendly vocabulary, add synonyms.
- *HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings)*: an LLM drafts a hypothetical answer, then embed *that* — document-to-document matching closes the question/answer embedding gap. Costs an extra generation and can hallucinate misleading queries on niche/factual domains (+25–60% latency on small models); use selectively when query–document similarity confidence is low.
- *Multi-query*: fan out into several paraphrases, retrieve for each, fuse.
- *Step-back prompting*: ask a more general question first to retrieve foundational context.
- *Sub-question decomposition*: break multi-hop questions into answerable pieces.

**Contextual retrieval (Anthropic, Sept 2024).** Before embedding/indexing, prepend to each chunk a short LLM-generated blurb situating it in the whole document (cheap via prompt caching, ~$1.02 per million document tokens). Reported top-20 retrieval failure-rate reductions: contextual embeddings alone −35% (5.7%→3.7%); + contextual BM25 −49% (→2.9%); reranked contextual embeddings + contextual BM25 −67% (→1.9%). A widely-cited critique notes the headline number is a best case and gains vary by corpus; still, it's now a default recommendation, and Anthropic found Gemini and Voyage embeddings particularly effective for it.

## Knowledge graphs and structured retrieval
**Microsoft GraphRAG.** Instead of embedding chunks, an LLM extracts entities and relationships into a knowledge graph, runs **Leiden community detection** hierarchically, and pre-generates community summaries at index time. Two query modes: **local search** (traverse a specific entity's neighborhood — entity-specific questions) and **global search** (map-reduce over community summaries — holistic "what are the main themes" questions vector RAG cannot answer). Later optimizations — DRIFT search and dynamic community selection — cut the expensive global map-reduce cost. GraphRAG wins on multi-hop reasoning, connecting disparate facts, and corpus-level sensemaking; costs substantially more to build/query (a 600-token indexing chunk extracted ~2× the entities of a 2400-token chunk in Microsoft's tests — a cost/completeness knob).

**Temporal knowledge-graph memory (Zep/Graphiti).** For agent memory, Graphiti (Zep, arXiv:2501.13956) builds a bi-temporal knowledge graph: each fact edge records *valid time* (when true in the world, valid_at/invalid_at) and *transaction time* (when the system learned it, created_at/expired_at), with EpisodicNodes preserving provenance. Directly addresses stale-memory failures and "what was true when" queries. Benchmarks contested (below), but the temporal-graph approach leads on temporal reasoning.

**Structured retrieval.** Metadata filtering, hybrid vector+graph traversal, text-to-SQL / text-to-query turn natural language into structured queries — the right tool when the question is "find all contracts with renewal dates before March 2025 where ARR > $200K," which is a filter, not a similarity search. Structure helps when relationships and constraints matter; hurts (pure overhead) when the corpus is unstructured prose and questions are lookup-style.

## Agentic retrieval — from static RAG to agent-driven retrieval
Classic RAG is a function: query in, retrieve once, generate once. **Agentic RAG** is a loop with a policy, with four primitives the static pipeline lacks: (1) *decision to retrieve* — answer trivial questions from parametric knowledge, only call the retriever when needed; (2) *query transformation* — rewrite/decompose before retrieving; (3) *iterative retrieval* — retrieve, read, decide if more evidence is needed, retrieve again, until sufficient or a step budget is hit; (4) *self-check* — a judge scores groundedness before the answer ships, looping back on failure. It enables backtracking: expand scope to a parent section, switch retrieval strategy, reformulate, or flag uncertainty rather than hallucinate. The trade: reliability on hard (multi-hop, ambiguous) questions for latency and tokens on easy ones; for single-doc lookups, classic RAG is still correct.

**Self-correcting retrieval.** *Self-RAG* (Asai et al., 2024) trains a model to emit reflection tokens — [Retrieve], [IsRel], [IsSup], [IsUse] — so it decides on-demand whether to retrieve, critiques passage relevance, and grades whether its output is supported. *CRAG (Corrective RAG)* (Yan et al., 2024) adds a lightweight retrieval evaluator grading documents Correct/Ambiguous/Incorrect and, when retrieval is poor, triggers corrective actions — knowledge refinement (strip irrelevant sentences) or fallback to web search. Distinction: CRAG bolts onto any LLM; Self-RAG requires instruction-tuning to produce its critic tokens. RL-trained variants (Search-R1, Search-o1) learn search policies; note the "task forgetting" / repeated-retrieval failure mode when planning is weak. Caution (arXiv:2512.10787): naive iterative retrieval that keeps *appending* passages causes unbounded context growth ("context dilution") — fixed-budget evidence-replacement strategies address this.

**Retrieval as tool use.** In the agent loop, retrieval is one tool among many; the agent chooses between memory retrieval, web search, code execution, etc. Increasingly exposed as an **MCP server** (Anthropic Nov 2024; a JSON-RPC client–server standard now adopted across OpenAI, Google, Cursor), giving any agent a standardized retrieval interface. Reasoning models change the calculus: the model can *reason* about what to retrieve, formulate better sub-queries, and evaluate evidence — making agentic retrieval markedly more effective than heuristic pipelines.

**Interaction with caching (CAG).** From the previous chapter: retrieved content is dynamic, so it belongs at the *end* of the prompt to preserve the cached prefix. **Cache-Augmented Generation (CAG)** takes the opposite bet: if the knowledge base is small and stable (Anthropic suggests under ~200k tokens / ~500 pages), preload it all into context and precompute the KV cache, eliminating retrieval latency and errors. The tension is real: CAG wins on small/stable corpora (speed, simplicity), RAG wins on large/dynamic ones — and CAG must rebuild its cache whenever documents change, so it fails for hourly-changing data. Hybrid CAG-RAG — cache the stable core, retrieve the long tail — is an emerging middle path.

## Retrieval failure modes and quality
**The precision/recall tension.** Retrieve too little → recall failure (miss relevant info). Retrieve too much → precision failure (pollute context with distractors, feeding context rot). The reranker exists precisely to let you cast a wide recall net then cut to a precise set.

**Specific failures.**
- *Semantic mismatch / the embedding gap*: embeddings capture similarity, not answerability — a chunk can be semantically near the query yet not answer it (a query is a question; documents are statements). HyDE and rerankers target this.
- *Lost in the middle* (Liu et al., TACL 2024, vol. 12, pp. 157–173; arXiv:2307.03172): LLM accuracy is U-shaped in the position of relevant info — "performance is often highest when relevant information occurs at the beginning or end of the input context, and significantly degrades when models must access relevant information in the middle," a ~30%+ drop on multi-document QA when the answer moves from position 1 to position 10 in a 20-document context. Replicated across GPT-3.5, GPT-4, Claude; the MIT 2025 follow-up attributes it to causal masking and RoPE long-term decay. Mitigations: rerank and truncate to few passages, place highest-relevance chunks at the edges, compress passages.
- *Stale/outdated info*: retrieving superseded facts — the motivation for temporal graphs and freshness filters.
- *Chunk-boundary problems*: relevant info split across chunks — addressed by overlap, parent-document retrieval, late chunking.
- *Needle-in-a-haystack*: finding one fact in a huge context; RULER shows most long-context models fail well below their advertised window.

**Evaluation.** IR metrics — recall@k, precision@k, MRR, NDCG@k — measure retrieval directly and need labeled relevance. For end-to-end RAG, **RAGAS** provides reference-free LLM-judged metrics: *faithfulness* (fraction of answer claims supported by context — catches hallucination), *answer relevance* (answer vs question), *context precision* (are relevant chunks ranked high, via average precision over per-chunk judgments), *context recall* (does retrieved context cover the ground truth, statement-by-statement against a reference). Discipline: context recall + context precision localize retrieval failures; faithfulness localizes generation failures (low faithfulness with good retrieval → the prompt or model is the problem). Track precision *and* recall together — you can game context precision by returning fewer chunks at the expense of recall. Ragas also offers agent metrics (tool-call accuracy, agent goal accuracy) and noise sensitivity. Others: ARES, TruLens. The deeper challenge: all LLM-judge metrics are sensitive to judge model and prompt — treat scores as directional and A/B against your own corpus.

## The frontier and ongoing debates (2026)
**Long context vs RAG.** Findings conflict because they depend on model capacity. The Databricks/Mosaic study (Leng, Portes, Havens, Zaharia & Carbin, arXiv:2411.03538; 20 LLMs, context grown 2k→128k and up to 2M, 512-token chunks/256-token stride, text-embedding-3-large) found retrieving more documents helps up to a point but longer context isn't always better — **Llama-3.1-405B degraded after 32k, GPT-4-0125-preview after 64k**, while only the strongest models (o1, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Claude 3 Opus) improved steadily toward ~100k; Gemini 1.5 Pro/Flash held to 2M. Distinct failure modes surfaced: Claude 3.5 Sonnet copyright refusals rose 3.7%→49.5% (16k→64k); DBRX instruction-following errors rose 5.2%→50.4% (8k→32k). Li et al. (arXiv:2501.01880): long-context models beat RAG on self-contained narrative text ('song', 'film', 'novel') while RAG excels on fragmented/technical corpora ('country', 'dataset', 'model'), and open-source models (weaker at long context) benefit more from retrieval while strong closed models do better with full context. Emerging consensus: **complementary, not competitive** — retrieve *into* long context ("RAG-augmented long context"). RAG remains indispensable for large corpora, dynamic data, cost, and latency; "RAG is dead" is mostly a social-media narrative production adoption contradicts (RAGFlow's 2025 year-end review: enterprises "cannot live without RAG, yet remain unsatisfied").

**Late-interaction models.** ColBERT (Khattab & Zaharia, 2020) stores a vector per token and scores via MaxSim (sum of max token-level similarities) — finer-grained matching than single-vector models, at an order-of-magnitude larger storage footprint. ColBERTv2 (2022) cut that cost via residual compression. Resurgent because it excels exactly where bi-encoders and cross-encoders struggle (identifier-heavy, exact-match, out-of-domain), often layered as a first stage with SPLADE candidate filtering.

**Multimodal / visual document retrieval.** **ColPali** (Faysse et al., ICLR 2025) extends late interaction to documents-as-images: SigLIP-So400m vision encoder + PaliGemma producing ~1,024 patch embeddings per page (32×32 grid, each projected to 128 dims), scored against query tokens via MaxSim, evaluated on ViDoRe (NDCG@5). Eliminates the brittle OCR+layout-parsing ingestion step for PDFs, tables, charts. The family now includes ColQwen and efficient small models (ColModernVBERT, ~250M params, within 0.6 NDCG@5 of ColPali; larger 4–8B multi-vector systems top quality but exceed 7s CPU query latency). Multimodal embeddings (Cohere Embed v4, Voyage) also encode text and images into one shared space.

**Memory-specific retrieval and its benchmark wars.** Retrieving from conversation history differs from document RAG: it demands temporal reasoning, relevance decay (knowing what still matters), and per-user scoping. Frameworks: **Mem0** (vector-first, optional graph; ~48k GitHub stars, reporting 66.9% LOCOMO accuracy at ~0.71s median latency using ~1,800 tokens/conversation vs a full-context baseline of 72.9% at 9.87s and ~26,000 tokens), **Zep/Graphiti** (temporal KG; ~63.8% vs Mem0's 49.0% on LongMemEval with GPT-4o per one independent test), **LangMem** (MIT-licensed, LangChain), **Letta** (formerly MemGPT). Numbers hotly disputed — Zep argues Mem0 benchmarked a misconfigured Zep (wrong user model, improper timestamp handling) and claims a corrected LOCOMO score of 75.14% vs the 65.99% Mem0 reported for it; Mem0 found Zep's memory footprint exceeds 600,000 tokens/conversation with correct answers sometimes surfacing only hours after ingestion. LOCOMO conversations are short (16–26k tokens, inside modern windows) and vendor eval harnesses differ (answer model, judge model, question subsets — a model swap alone moved scores ~10 points), so scores don't transfer to long agentic tasks. Newer benchmarks (LongMemEval, LoCoMo-Plus, MemoryArena) aim to fix this. Bottom line: treat vendor scores as directional; Mem0 leads on personalization/latency, Zep on temporal queries.

**Fine-tuned embeddings.** Domain fine-tuning reportedly yields +10–30% on specialized domains (legal, medical, code); specialized Voyage models cited as beating generic models by 10–15% in-domain. As embedding costs fall (Gemini Embedding at $0.15/1M tokens; some open models near-free self-hosted), fine-tuning the retriever is increasingly the highest-leverage quality investment.

## Recommendations (staged)
0. **Start simple and measure.** Recursive ~512-token chunks (10–20% overlap), a strong general embedding model (text-embedding-3-large or open Qwen3/BGE), HNSW cosine, top-k=10. Immediately stand up a RAGAS harness with a labeled question set from your real corpus. Don't optimize what you're not measuring.
1. **Add hybrid + reranking early (highest ROI).** Add BM25/SPLADE, fuse with RRF (k=60; lower for small corpora), retrieve 50–200 candidates, rerank (Cohere Rerank 3.5 or bge-reranker-v2-m3) to top 3–10. Biggest precision win for least effort. Aim for context recall > 0.8 and rising context precision.
2. **Fix chunking and queries where metrics point.** Low context recall → the retriever is missing content: add contextual retrieval on high-value documents, try late chunking for reference-heavy text, add query rewriting/decomposition for multi-hop. Low faithfulness with high recall → the problem is the prompt/model, not retrieval.
3. **Go agentic when questions are hard.** Multi-hop, ambiguous, or freshness-requiring → wrap retrieval in an agentic loop (decision-to-retrieve, iterative retrieval, CRAG-style evaluation with web-search fallback, groundedness self-check). Expose retrieval as an MCP tool so the agent composes it with web search. Keep a step budget; use fixed-budget evidence replacement, not unbounded appending, to avoid context dilution.
4. **Add structure only if it earns its keep.** GraphRAG for corpus-level sensemaking / multi-hop fact-connecting; a temporal graph (Graphiti) for agent memory where facts change and "what was true when" matters. Both cost materially more — justify with a benchmark, since (per Mem0's own data) the graph often barely beats vectors.

**Build vs buy.** Roll your own on a vector DB (Qdrant/pgvector/Turbopuffer) when you need full control, custom/fine-tuned embeddings, or hybrid+metadata filtering. Use a managed framework (Mem0/Zep/LangMem) to move fast on conversational personalization — accepting that most route your data through their LLMs and publish easily-overfit benchmarks. For small stable KBs, consider CAG (preload + KV cache) and skip retrieval.

**Three-lever synthesis.** Retrieval (this chapter), prompt caching (previous chapter), and context management combine: cache the stable prefix (system prompt, tool defs, and — if small — the whole KB via CAG), place *dynamic retrieved content at the end* to preserve the cached prefix, and use reranking + tight top-k to keep the working set small enough to dodge context rot and lost-in-the-middle.

**Thresholds that change the plan.** Single-shot → agentic retrieval when multi-hop questions exceed ~20% of traffic. Vector → hybrid the moment exact-match queries (codes, names) appear. HNSW-in-Postgres → dedicated engine past ~50M vectors. RAG → CAG when the corpus is under ~200k tokens and stable. Text → visual/late-interaction (ColPali) when the corpus is PDFs, tables, and charts rather than clean text.

## Caveats
- This field moves fast; specifics change monthly. Embedding leaderboards, reranker versions, and vector-DB benchmarks are moving targets — the MTEB leaderboard itself shifted from v1 to v2 (scores not directly comparable). Re-verify model specs; always benchmark on *your* data.
- Vendor and benchmark claims are contested: Anthropic's 67% figure is a best case; the Mem0/Zep LOCOMO dispute is unresolved; LLM-as-judge metrics (including RAGAS) are sensitive to judge model and prompt. Treat all single-number claims as directional.
- Many roundup sources are marketing: several 2026 "leaderboard" and embedding-comparison pages are SEO content with unverifiable numbers; this chapter relies on primary papers and official docs where possible and flags aggregator-only figures (e.g., precise MTEB scores for Cohere Embed v4 and Gemini weren't confirmable from vendors' own pages, though Gemini's "#1 MTEB multilingual" claim is official).
- Some cited benchmark numbers are corpus-specific (WANDS, financial QA, ViDoRe, LOCOMO) and won't reproduce on different data — they illustrate direction and magnitude, not guarantees.
